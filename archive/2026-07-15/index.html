Bitcoin Gains After "Much Better-Than-Expected" US PPI Print

Bitcoin surged to $65,500 on Wednesday — its highest since June 22 — after the June Producer Price Index came in cooler than expected at 5.5% year-on-year, following a 0.3% monthly decline, according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The print follows Tuesday's downside surprise in CPI, which had already boosted risk assets despite macro pressure from the US-Iran conflict and its impact on oil prices. Economist Mohamed El-Erian called the figures "much better-than-expected" and said they are "set to boost equities and further temper market expectations for upcoming interest rate hikes." The CME FedWatch Tool now shows a rate hike in September is no longer the most likely scenario, marking a significant shift in sentiment. However, traders remained cautious about Bitcoin's ability to sustain its gains, noting bear-market history and local resistance levels above $65,000.

Three Democratic Senators Oppose CLARITY Act on Ethics Grounds Ahead of Key Vote

Senate Majority Leader John Thune has said the chamber will consider a vote on the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act before August 10, but the bill faces headwinds from Democrats demanding stronger conflict-of-interest provisions. Senators Chris Murphy, Jeff Merkley and Chris Van Hollen held a press conference alongside actor Ben McKenzie and consumer advocacy groups, arguing the bill does not address what they called "Trump's crypto corruption" — referring to the president's ties to World Liberty Financial and his memecoin ventures. DeFi Security

Ostium Pauses Trading After Apparent $18M–$22M Oracle Exploit

Decentralized trading protocol Ostium halted all trading on Wednesday after blockchain security firms Blockaid and CertiK reported an apparent exploit of its OLP liquidity vault, with estimated losses ranging from $18 million to $22 million. Both firms attributed the incident to a compromise of Ostium's oracle system, which supplies external price data to the protocol. Ostium advised all users to "temporarily revoke approvals" for its contracts while the team investigates. Built on Arbitrum, Ostium is an onchain perpetuals trading platform offering leveraged exposure to 75 trading pairs. The incident is the latest in a series of high-profile DeFi attacks this year, with April alone seeing nearly $630 million in losses — the highest monthly total since February 2025.

Crypto Firms Face AML Risks During Post-MiCA Migration, Warns AMLA Chair

Mass user migration following the end of MiCA's 18-month transitional period on July 1 could strain compliance at virtual asset service providers in the European Union, according to Bruna Szego, chair of the EU's Anti-Money Laundering Authority. "Because we know customers will rush to withdraw, this will put additional pressure on these VASPs," Szego said during a briefing with the European Parliament's Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs. Firms winding down their EU operations face pressure as customers rush to withdraw funds, while licensed crypto companies could face onboarding challenges as they absorb new users. The European Securities and Markets Authority has said crypto service providers that remain unauthorized must take "immediate" steps to wind down their EU activities.

Tech AI

Over 95% of Coinbase's Code Now Written with Help of AI, Says Platform Head

Artificial intelligence now helps write more than 95% of Coinbase's code, according to platform head Rob Witoff, more than doubling the company's February estimate of 40%. Coinbase's May layoffs of 700 staff were tied in part to this shift, with CEO Brian Armstrong saying AI has "dramatically changed the pace of work." Witoff told Cointelegraph that nearly 100% of employees use AI daily, with most engineers running five to 10 AI agents at any given time. By 2030, he projected, AI agents could do the equivalent work of 100,000 employees. While core cryptography development still relies heavily on human expertise, prototyping is now "effectively 100% automated," and teams of two to three senior employees now handle work that previously required 10 or more people.

DeFi Lending

Aave Launches V4 on Avalanche, Paving Way for Tokenized Credit Markets

Decentralized lending protocol Aave has deployed V4 on Avalanche, marking the first expansion of its latest lending infrastructure beyond Ethereum. The launch introduces Aave's Hub & Spoke architecture, which allows specialized lending markets to operate with their own collateral requirements while drawing on shared liquidity. One of the first planned markets on Avalanche will support borrowing against tokenized assets, including US Treasurys, money market funds, private credit and corporate bonds — each with customized risk parameters. Aave remains the largest DeFi lending protocol with nearly $14 billion in total value locked across 23 blockchains, according to DeFiLlama. The deployment comes as financial institutions like Franklin Templeton, Nasdaq, and DTCC build infrastructure for tokenized asset collateral management.

✦ Analysis: Trump's AG Pick Faces Senate Grilling Over Crypto Pardon ✦

President Donald Trump's nominee for US Attorney General, Todd Blanche, faced sharp questioning from both sides of the aisle during his Senate Judiciary Committee confirmation hearing over his role in reshaping the Justice Department's crypto enforcement strategy and the presidential pardon of former Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao. Senator Dick Durbin accused Blanche of dismantling the Justice Department's crypto enforcement unit and ending active investigations into the digital asset sector, arguing the shift benefited Trump's crypto interests including World Liberty Financial. Republican Senator Thom Tillis also raised concerns about Zhao's pardon following his 2023 guilty plea over Anti-Money Laundering violations. Blanche rejected no allegations directly but said he would review the pardon process if confirmed. The hearing underscores the deepening politicization of crypto enforcement in Washington, as the CLARITY Act heads toward a Senate vote and the administration's crypto-friendly policies face mounting scrutiny from lawmakers on both sides of the aisle.
